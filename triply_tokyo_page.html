The Suica card — get one first thing At Narita or Haneda airport, get a Suica or Pasmo IC card. It covers every train, subway, bus and monorail in Tokyo — and you can also use it at vending machines, convenience stores and taxis. Tap in, tap out. No tickets, no queues. Tokyo's train system is legendarily complex but with a Suica and Google Maps, it becomes simple.
Tokyo Travel Guide

Temples, Ramen, Neon
& Everything In Between

Tokyo is not one city — it's dozens of neighborhoods stacked together, each with its own rules. This guide helps you find the ones that matter to you.

Why Tokyo?

Tokyo has more Michelin-starred restaurants than any other city on earth (230+). It also has a 7-Eleven onigiri for ¥150 that's genuinely better than most convenience store food anywhere else. Both of these things are true, and that's the key to understanding Tokyo.

It's a city that takes craft seriously at every level. Ramen, sushi, tempura, the way a train door opens exactly where the platform marking says it will — there's a precision and attention to detail here that you feel constantly, even when you can't name it.

Tokyo is the only city in the world where you can eat extraordinarily well for ¥800 and extraordinarily well for ¥80,000 — and both experiences will stay with you.

Asakusa — Old Tokyo

Senso-ji Temple is Tokyo's oldest temple, founded in 645 AD — older than the written history of the city. The approach through the Kaminarimon (Thunder Gate) with its giant red lantern, along the Nakamise shopping street to the temple proper, is the most atmospheric walk in Tokyo.

Visit before 8am or after 8pm — when the tour groups thin out and the temple grounds take on a completely different weight. The giant red lantern at night, lit up and almost empty, is one of Tokyo's best quiet moments.

💡 The temple hall itself is free to enter. Buy a fortune (omikuji) for ¥100 — if it's bad luck, tie it to the rack and leave it behind.

Shibuya — The Crossing & Beyond

The Shibuya Scramble Crossing is one of the most photographed intersections in the world — and it earns it. When the light turns, up to 3,000 people cross simultaneously from all directions. Watch it from the Starbucks above, from the Shibuya Sky observation deck, or simply stand in the middle of it.

Beyond the crossing: the Hachiko statue (the loyal dog), Shibuya 109 fashion building, and the backstreets of Daikanyama and Nakameguro — two of Tokyo's most stylish neighbourhoods, 10 minutes walk south.

💡 Shibuya Sky rooftop observation deck has some of the best views in Tokyo — book in advance, especially at sunset.

Shinjuku — The City That Never Sleeps

Shinjuku contains multitudes. The east exit has Kabukicho — Tokyo's entertainment district, neon-soaked and slightly chaotic. Golden Gai — a network of tiny alleys with over 200 bars, most fitting 5–8 people. Omoide Yokocho (Memory Lane) — a narrow alley of yakitori stalls, smoke and laughter, open until late.

The west exit has the Tokyo Metropolitan Government Building — free observation deck, one of the best views in the city, open until 11pm most nights.

Golden Gai at 10pm on a weekday — find a bar, order whatever they're pouring, start talking to whoever's there. This is Tokyo at its best.

Yanaka — The Neighbourhood That Survived

Yanaka escaped the WWII bombing and the postwar redevelopment that flattened most of old Tokyo. What remains: narrow lanes, wooden shopfronts, family-run businesses, temples and a cemetery that locals walk through like a park. Yanaka Ginza shopping street has local food, crafts and one of the most genuinely old-Tokyo atmospheres you'll find.

Come here when you've had enough of neon. It's a 10-minute walk from Nippori station.

Tsukiji Outer Market — The Sushi Breakfast

The famous inner wholesale market moved to Toyosu in 2018, but the outer market — rows of small stalls and restaurants surrounding it — remains active and excellent. Come before 10am for the freshest sushi, tamagoyaki (sweet rolled omelette) and shellfish.

A sushi breakfast at one of the counter restaurants here (¥1,500–¥3,000) is one of the best Tokyo experiences at any price point. Order omakase, let the chef decide, eat what's in front of you.

💡 Arrive before 9am. It gets crowded fast. Bring cash — many stalls are cash only.

What to Eat in Tokyo

Tokyo's food culture is the most serious and diverse on the planet. These are the things worth making time for:

🍜 Ramen — find a specialty shop, not a chain. Tonkotsu (creamy pork broth), shoyu (soy), or shio (salt). Standing counter or queue for a seat. Budget ¥800–1,200.
🍣 Sushi — from kaiten (conveyor belt, ¥110–¥220/plate) to standing sushi bars in Tsukiji (¥150–¥400/piece) to full omakase. All of it is excellent.
🍢 Yakitori — grilled chicken skewers, every part of the bird. Order with cold beer. Omoide Yokocho in Shinjuku is the right place.
🍱 Convenience store food — genuinely. 7-Eleven, FamilyMart and Lawson in Japan are a different category from anywhere else. Onigiri, sandwiches, hot foods — try them.
🍡 Depachika — department store basement food halls. Isetan in Shinjuku and Mitsukoshi in Ginza have more variety than most cities' entire food scenes. Go to browse even if you don't buy.
🍵 Matcha everything — Asakusa and Ueno area have excellent traditional wagashi (sweets) shops. Pair with a cup of green tea.

Best Time to Visit Tokyo

🌸 Spring (late March–May) — cherry blossom season. Ueno Park, Shinjuku Gyoen, Meguro River. Busiest and most expensive — book 3–4 months ahead.
🍂 Autumn (October–November) — best overall. Fall colours, comfortable weather, fewer crowds than spring.
❄️ Winter (December–February) — cold but clear. Mt. Fuji views are best. New Year celebrations are exceptional.
☀️ Summer (June–August) — hot, humid, typhoon season. Vibrant festivals (matsuri) and fireworks displays.

2026 note: Weak yen means 25–30% effective discount for EUR and USD holders. Excellent time to visit.

Tokyo Travel Tips

💳 Suica / Pasmo card — get one at the airport. Use it for everything: trains, buses, convenience stores, vending machines.
📱 Get a Japan SIM or eSIM at the airport — Google Maps + Google Translate (camera mode for menus) makes Tokyo infinitely easier.
💴 Carry cash — many small restaurants, temples and market stalls are cash only. ATMs at 7-Eleven and Japan Post accept foreign cards.
👟 You will walk 15,000+ steps per day. Comfortable shoes are not optional.
🎌 Basic etiquette — don't eat while walking, be quiet on trains, queue properly. You'll figure it out quickly — Tokyo is forgiving of honest mistakes.
🗾 Tokyo is huge — one "neighbourhood" can take 20+ minutes to cross. Plan around the train, not walking distance.

⚠️ Underestimating distances

Tokyo is enormous. "Nearby neighbourhoods" can be 30+ minutes by train. Plan around the train lines — use Google Maps Tokyo transit mode for every journey.

⚠️ Narita vs Haneda

Narita Airport (NRT) is 60km from central Tokyo — 1 hour+ by train. Haneda (HND) is 30 minutes. Check which airport your flight uses — it matters for your first and last day.

Where to eat
Tokyo food picks
230+ Michelin stars. But the best meals are often the cheapest. Both are true.
🍜
Fuunji — Tsukemen Ramen
📍 Shinjuku · One of the best tsukemen (dipping ramen) in Tokyo. Thick, rich broth, chewy noodles. Small counter bar, queue outside. Opens at 11am — arrive early. Cash only, ¥900–1,100. This is the kind of place Tokyo does better than anywhere else on earth.
📍 Maps →
🍣
Tsukiji Outer Market Sushi
📍 Tsukiji · Several counter sushi restaurants along the outer market stalls. Arrive before 10am. Order omakase (chef's choice) or point at what you want. ¥1,500–¥3,000 for a full breakfast. The fish quality is as good as it gets. One of the best value meals in Tokyo.
📍 Maps →
🍢
Omoide Yokocho — Memory Lane
📍 Shinjuku West Exit · A narrow alley of tiny yakitori bars that has barely changed since the 1950s. Smoke, skewers, cold beer, salarymen and tourists side by side. Order chicken yakitori, gizzards, vegetables. ¥300–¥500 per skewer. Go at 8pm on any weekday. One of Tokyo's most atmospheric experiences.
📍 Maps →
🍵
Nakamise-dori Snacks — Asakusa
📍 Asakusa · The 250-metre shopping street leading to Senso-ji has excellent traditional snacks — ningyo-yaki (small cakes shaped like traditional figures), senbei rice crackers, matcha soft-serve, and melonpan. Budget ¥200–¥500 per item. Eat while walking the approach to the temple — tourist but worth it.
📍 Maps →
🏬
Depachika — the basement food halls Every major department store in Tokyo has a basement food floor (depachika) that is a destination in its own right. Isetan in Shinjuku and Mitsukoshi in Ginza are the best. Fresh pastries, bento boxes, prepared foods, wagashi sweets, imported cheese, premium sushi — more variety than most cities' entire food scenes. Go to browse and graze even if you're not hungry.
