Bali Travel Guide

Temples, Rice Terraces, Beaches
& the Soul of the Island

Bali is bigger and more varied than most visitors realise. The key is knowing which area to base yourself in — because each part of the island feels like a different country.

Why Bali?

Bali is Indonesia's only Hindu island in a predominantly Muslim country — and that spiritual difference permeates everything. Daily offerings (canang sari) of flowers, incense and food appear on doorsteps, shop counters and temple entrances every morning. Ceremonies happen constantly, in streets and temples and rice fields. The culture is alive, not preserved.

And then there's the sheer variety: ancient temples, lush rice terraces with UNESCO recognition, active volcanoes you can hike for sunrise, world-class surf breaks, white sand beaches and cliff-top infinity pools. Almost any kind of traveller finds what they came for in Bali.

The paradise is real. It just requires knowing where to look.

Ubud — Bali's Cultural Heart

Ubud is the cultural capital of Bali — set in the green hills of central Bali, surrounded by rice terraces, rivers and forest. Temples, art galleries, traditional dance performances, cooking classes and some of Bali's best restaurants are all concentrated here.

The Tegallalang Rice Terraces are 10 minutes north of Ubud — the iconic emerald stepped terraces that represent Bali's UNESCO-recognised subak irrigation system. Go early morning (7am) before the tour groups arrive and the heat builds.

Sacred Monkey Forest — 700 long-tailed macaques living in a forest sanctuary with three ancient Hindu temples. Extraordinary atmosphere; keep your belongings secure.

💡 Ubud's best experiences are early morning — rice terrace walks, temple visits, markets. By 10am the crowds have arrived. Set your alarm and go first.

Uluwatu — Cliffs, Surf & the Kecak Fire Dance

Uluwatu on Bali's southern Bukit Peninsula is one of the island's most dramatic areas — towering limestone cliffs, hidden beaches accessible only by long staircases, world-class surf breaks (Padang Padang, Uluwatu, Bingin), and cliff-top beach clubs with infinity pool views over the Indian Ocean.

The Uluwatu Temple (Pura Luhur Uluwatu) perches on a cliff edge 70 metres above the ocean. The Kecak Fire Dance performed here at sunset — 50+ men chanting in rotating rhythms, firelight, the ocean below — is one of the most spectacular performances in Bali. Book in advance, arrive 30 minutes early for the sunset view.

💡 Beware of the macaque monkeys at Uluwatu Temple — they will grab glasses, phones and bags. Keep everything secured and inside your pockets or bag.

Mount Batur — Sunrise Above the Clouds

Mount Batur (1,717m) is Bali's most accessible volcano trek — a 2-hour hike starting at 2–3am to reach the summit for sunrise. Standing above the clouds with the caldera lake below and the rising sun turning everything gold is one of the most memorable experiences on the island.

A local guide is required (mandatory for the trek). The hike is moderate — no technical climbing required. Bring warm layers — the summit is cold before sunrise.

💡 Book your Batur sunrise trek with a reputable guide company the day before. Start time is typically 1–3am depending on the guide. Bring a headtorch, warm jacket and water.

Nusa Penida — The Wild Island

Nusa Penida is a 30-minute fast boat from Sanur — wilder, more dramatic and less developed than mainland Bali. The Kelingking Beach viewpoint (T-Rex cliff shaped headland above turquoise water) is one of the most photographed spots in all of Indonesia. Angel's Billabong and Broken Beach are extraordinary natural formations.

Nusa Penida requires at least one overnight stay to see it properly — day trips are possible but rushed. The roads are rough, scooters are the best way around. Plan for at least 2 nights.

What to Eat in Bali

Balinese cuisine is one of the great underrated food cultures of Asia — aromatic, spiced and rooted in Hindu ceremonial traditions. Don't miss the local food in favour of the international cafés.

🐷 Babi Guling — spit-roasted suckling pig stuffed with turmeric, lemongrass and spices. Bali's most celebrated dish (note: not available everywhere as pork is not eaten by Muslims — Bali is Hindu). Ibu Oka in Ubud is the most famous version.
🍚 Nasi Campur — "mixed rice", served with small portions of various side dishes. The everyday Balinese meal. Try it at a warung (local restaurant) for under IDR 30,000 (~€2).
🥜 Satay (Sate) — grilled skewers of chicken, pork or seafood with peanut sauce. Available everywhere, best from a local warung or street stall.
🍜 Mie Goreng / Nasi Goreng — fried noodles or fried rice, the staple Indonesian dishes. Ubiquitous, always good, always cheap.
🌿 Jamu — traditional Balinese herbal drink made with turmeric, ginger and honey. Sold by women in traditional dress carrying baskets. The original wellness drink.
☕ Kopi Bali — Balinese coffee grown in the highlands around Kintamani. Strong, served with grounds in the cup. Also available as the famous (but controversial) luwak coffee.
⚠️ The international cafés and smoothie bowl spots of Canggu and Seminyak are good but don't represent Balinese food. Make time to eat at a warung — cheaper, more authentic and genuinely delicious.

Best Time to Visit Bali

☀️ Dry season (Apr–Oct) — best weather, clear skies, ideal for beaches and hiking. July–August is peak season — book well in advance.
🌧️ Wet season (Nov–Mar) — daily rain showers (usually afternoon), lower prices, fewer crowds, lush green landscapes. Still very enjoyable with the right mindset.
🌸 April–June · September — the sweet spot. Dry weather, before or after the peak crowds, best prices.
🎭 Nyepi (Balinese New Year) — the most extraordinary cultural experience. The entire island goes silent for 24 hours — no lights, no movement, no noise. Usually March. Book months ahead.

Bali Travel Tips

🛵 Scooter or driver — within one area, Gojek scooter taxi. For day trips across Bali, hire a private car with driver (USD 45–55/day). Scooter rental is cheap but requires an international licence.
👗 Temple dress code — cover shoulders and knees at temples. Sarongs are provided at the entrance to major temples, but bring your own for smaller ones.
💵 Cash is king — carry Indonesian Rupiah. Many warungs, markets and small businesses are cash only. ATMs are widely available in tourist areas.
🌊 Bali belly — drink bottled water only, even for brushing teeth in some areas. Activated charcoal tablets (Norit brand) are the local remedy if you get sick.
📱 Local SIM or eSIM — get one at the airport. Data is essential for Gojek, Google Maps and WhatsApp communication with local businesses.
🙏 Respect the culture — don't step on or photograph offerings. Don't point feet at sacred objects. Dress respectfully near temples. Bali's culture is genuine and deserves genuine respect.

⚠️ Staying in Kuta

Kuta was once Bali's party hub but is now generally considered overdeveloped and past its prime. Seminyak, Canggu or Ubud offer much better experiences at similar or lower prices.

⚠️ Underestimating Bali's distances

Bali's roads are congested. Ubud to Uluwatu can take 2+ hours. Plan your trip by area — don't try to cover the whole island in short daily drives. Pick two or three bases and stay longer.

Where to eat
Bali food picks
From local warungs at IDR 30,000 to world-class fine dining. Bali's food scene is extraordinary at every level.
🐷
Ibu Oka — Babi Guling
📍 Jl. Suweta 2, Ubud · Bali's most famous babi guling restaurant — the spit-roasted suckling pig that Anthony Bourdain made famous. The crispy skin, turmeric-spiced meat and blood sausage served with rice and vegetables. Arrives sold out by 2pm most days. Arrive at opening (11am) for the best cuts. IDR 65,000–85,000. Cash only.
📍 Maps →
🌊
Jimbaran Seafood — Beach Dinner
📍 Jimbaran Beach · At dusk, the Jimbaran beachfront fills with lantern-lit tables directly on the sand. Fresh fish, prawns, lobster and squid grilled over coconut husks. Choose your fish from the display, they weigh it and grill it. Sunset, sea breeze and fresh seafood — one of the most atmospheric dinners in Bali. Go at 6pm for the sunset.
📍 Maps →
🌿
Locavore — Fine Dining Ubud
📍 Jl. Dewi Sita, Ubud · Consistently ranked among Asia's best restaurants — modern Indonesian cuisine using entirely local ingredients. The tasting menu changes seasonally and showcases Balinese produce in ways you won't find anywhere else. Book weeks in advance for dinner. The Locavore To Go casual concept next door is a more affordable introduction.
📍 Maps →
☕
Seniman Coffee — Ubud
📍 Jl. Sri Wedari 5, Ubud · Bali's finest specialty coffee — single-origin Balinese beans roasted in-house, exceptional brews, beautiful garden setting. A morning coffee here before heading to the rice terraces is one of Ubud's best simple pleasures. Also serves excellent food. A benchmark for Bali's now-excellent café culture.
📍 Maps →
🍚
Eat at a warung — the real Balinese food experience A warung is a simple family-run Balinese restaurant. Nasi campur (mixed rice with side dishes), mie goreng, satay — authentic, freshly cooked, IDR 20,000–40,000 (~€1–2.50). Most tourists eat only at the international cafés and miss this entirely. The best warungs have no English sign, a handwritten menu and a local family cooking in the back. Ask your accommodation to point you to their favourite.
