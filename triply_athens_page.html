Athens Travel Guide

Ancient Ruins, Rooftop Bars
& the City Below the Parthenon

Athens is more than a stopover before the Greek islands. It's one of Europe's most rewarding cities — ancient, chaotic, walkable and full of great food.

Why Athens?

Athens is where Western civilisation was invented. Democracy, philosophy, theatre, the Olympic Games — all of it began here, on these hills, in these streets. The Parthenon has been standing for 2,500 years and it still commands the city's skyline.

But Athens is not a museum. It's a living, chaotic, creative city with a genuine food scene, brilliant rooftop bars, and neighbourhoods that have nothing to do with ancient history. The mistake most visitors make is treating it as a quick stopover. Two to three days is the minimum to feel the city properly.

Don't rush Athens. Walk into Plaka with no plan. Get lost. Find a taverna. That's where it begins.

The Acropolis — Still the Best Thing

After 2,500 years, the Acropolis remains the most impressive ancient site in the world. The Parthenon, the Erechtheion with its Caryatid columns, the Propylaea gateway — the scale and the setting are extraordinary. On a clear day you can see the sea from the top.

Book the 8am entry slot — this is important. The site in the morning has a different quality: cooler, quieter, the light is golden and the crowds haven't arrived. By 11am in summer it's a wall of tour groups and 40°C heat. Get there early and you'll have a completely different experience.

💡 The Acropolis Museum at the base of the hill is world-class — allow 2 hours. The original Caryatids are here (one is in the British Museum, controversially). The rooftop café has Parthenon views and is genuinely good.

Ancient Agora — The Hidden Gem

Most visitors go to the Acropolis and skip the Ancient Agora — Socrates' actual neighbourhood, where Athenian democracy was debated and practiced. This is a mistake. The Agora is less crowded, more intimate, and in many ways more interesting than the Acropolis. The Temple of Hephaestus here is one of the best-preserved ancient Greek temples in the world — more complete than the Parthenon.

Walk through it slowly. This is where Socrates walked. Where Pericles gave speeches. Where the concept of democracy was argued over for the first time in human history.

Monastiraki & Plaka — The Heart

Monastiraki is Athens at its most energetic — the famous flea market (best on Sunday mornings), street food, the Tzisdarakis Mosque turned ceramics museum, and some of the best rooftop bars in the city looking directly at the Acropolis. Have a coffee here and look up. That view never gets old.

Plaka is the oldest neighbourhood in Athens — narrow streets, neoclassical architecture, traditional tavernas, bougainvillea on whitewashed walls. It's touristy, but it's touristy because it's genuinely beautiful. Walk through it without a strict plan and let it happen.

💡 The best Acropolis view in Athens is from the rooftop of A for Athens hotel in Monastiraki. You don't have to be a guest — the rooftop bar is open to everyone. Go at sunset.

Koukaki — Where Athens Actually Lives

Koukaki is the neighbourhood emerging as Athens' most interesting — at the southern base of the Acropolis, largely left alone by tourists. Excellent tavernas and cafés, the Acropolis Museum a 10-minute walk, a real neighbourhood feel. The streets along Drakou Street have some of the best brunch spots and modern tavernas in the city.

Stay here if you want to feel like you're in Athens, not in a tourist version of Athens.

Views — Where to See the City

Mount Lycabettus — the highest point in Athens, reached by funicular or a 20-minute walk up. The view from the top is the best in the city: the Acropolis below you, the sea in the distance, the entire basin of Athens spread out. Go at sunset.

Filopappou Hill — quieter and less known than Lycabettus. A 15-minute walk from Plaka. The view of the Acropolis from here — with the city below — is one of the best angles in Athens.

Anafiotika — a tiny hidden neighbourhood built into the rock of the Acropolis, looking like a Cycladic island village dropped into central Athens. Find it by walking up through Plaka. Most visitors walk straight past the entrance.

What to Eat in Athens

Greek food at its best is simple, fresh and honest. Athens has excellent tavernas at every price point.

🥙 Souvlaki — pork or chicken skewers, wrapped in pita with tomato, onion and tzatziki. The best fast food in Europe. Budget €3–5.
🧆 Mezedes — small sharing plates: taramosalata, tzatziki, dolmades, saganaki (fried cheese), grilled octopus. Order several, eat slowly.
🐙 Grilled octopus — chargrilled, drizzled with olive oil and lemon. One of the great simple dishes. Available everywhere near Monastiraki and the Agora.
🥗 Horiatiki (Greek salad) — tomato, cucumber, olives, feta, olive oil. In Athens, made with the right ingredients, it's revelatory.
🍯 Loukoumades — small fried dough balls drenched in honey and sesame. The original Greek street food, sold in speciality shops.
☕ Greek coffee / frappé — Greek coffee (ellinikos kafes) served unfiltered in a small cup. Frappé (iced instant coffee) is the summer staple. Both are drunk slowly.
⚠️ Avoid the tavernas directly on Monastiraki Square and Adrianou Street in Plaka — tourist menus, tourist prices. Walk two streets away and the quality and price improve immediately.

Best Time to Visit Athens

🌸 Spring (April–June) — best weather, wildflowers, comfortable temperatures for walking ancient sites
🍂 Autumn (Sep–Oct) — ideal. Still warm, fewer crowds than summer, excellent food scene
☀️ Summer (Jul–Aug) — very hot (35–40°C), crowded, but beaches accessible and nightlife excellent
❄️ Winter (Nov–Mar) — quiet, mild, cheap. Many sites nearly empty. Good for museums and food without the crowds.

Athens Travel Tips

🎫 Book Acropolis tickets in advance — mandatory timed entry. Don't arrive without a booking.
🚇 Metro — clean, efficient, covers most central areas. €1.20 per ride or €4.50 for a day pass. Line 3 (blue) goes direct to the airport.
👟 Athens is very walkable in the historic centre — Plaka, Monastiraki, Syntagma and Koukaki are all connected on foot. But wear comfortable shoes — cobblestones everywhere.
🌊 Athens Riviera — 30 minutes from the centre by tram or metro. Clear blue water, beach bars, swimming. A perfect afternoon between sightseeing days.
⛵ Greek Islands — Piraeus port is 30 minutes from central Athens. Hydra, Aegina and Poros are 1–2 hours by ferry. Easy day trips or overnight stays.

⚠️ Staying in the suburbs to save money

Athens' suburbs are far from the centre. The time and taxi costs outweigh any hotel savings. Stay within walking distance of Monastiraki or Syntagma.

⚠️ Skipping Athens for the islands

Athens deserves at least 2–3 days, not one rushed morning. The Acropolis alone needs 3 hours. The food scene, neighbourhoods and nightlife are genuinely world-class.

Where to eat
Athens food picks
Simple, fresh, honest. Greek food at its best doesn't try hard — it just is.
🥙
Kostas — Souvlaki Since 1950
📍 Adrianou 116, Monastiraki · A tiny souvlaki counter that has been feeding Athens for over 70 years. Pork souvlaki in pita, tomato, onion, paprika. €3. Queue outside, eat standing. One of the great cheap meals in Europe. Open only until they sell out — arrive by 1pm.

Taverna tou Psara — Plaka
📍 Erechtheos 16, Plaka · One of Plaka's best traditional tavernas — tucked into the hillside with steps leading up to a terrace. Grilled octopus, fresh fish, mezedes. The kind of place that rewards lingering. Lunch is more relaxed than dinner. Reserve for evening in high season.

Lukumades — Honey Doughnuts
📍 Theatrou Square, Monastiraki · The best loukoumades in Athens — small fried dough balls, piping hot, drenched in Greek thyme honey, cinnamon and sesame. The original Greek street dessert. Queue at the window, eat on the square. €3–5 for a portion. Don't skip this.

Nolan — Modern Greek
📍 Voulis 11, Syntagma · Modern Greek cuisine in a relaxed setting near Syntagma Square. Creative takes on traditional ingredients — excellent for those who want to go beyond the taverna. Good natural wine list. Book ahead for dinner. One of the best mid-range restaurants in central Athens.

☕
Greek coffee culture — sit, don't rush Greeks drink coffee slowly and for a long time. A frappe (iced instant coffee, shaken until frothy) can last an hour. Ordering a coffee in Athens means you have the table. No one will rush you. Find a café with an Acropolis view, order a frappe or a Greek coffee, and sit. This is not a small thing — it's how Athens works.
