Singapore Travel Guide

Futuristic Skyline, Four Cultures
& the Best Food in Asia

Singapore is compact, efficient and endlessly surprising. Most visitors scratch the surface. This guide helps you find the depth.

Why Singapore?

Singapore is one of the cleanest, safest and most efficient cities on earth — a city-state of 5.9 million people on an island smaller than London. It shouldn't work as well as it does, and yet it works extraordinarily well.

What makes it special: the food. Singapore has more culinary variety per square kilometre than almost anywhere in Asia. Chinese, Malay, Indian and Peranakan cooking exist side by side — at hawker stalls, in neighbourhood restaurants, in Michelin-starred kitchens. You can eat brilliantly for S$4 or S$400 and both experiences are worth having.

Singapore is the only city where a S$4 hawker stall and a Michelin-starred restaurant can occupy the same building. And both are worth visiting.

Marina Bay — The Postcard

Marina Bay is Singapore's showcase — the Marina Bay Sands hotel with its iconic rooftop infinity pool, the lotus-shaped ArtScience Museum, the Esplanade performing arts complex, and the spectacular Gardens by the Bay with its giant Supertrees.

The Garden Rhapsody light and sound show at Gardens by the Bay happens nightly at 7:45pm and 8:45pm — free, standing in the Supertree Grove, the trees lit up in colour and music. One of Singapore's best free experiences.

💡 The Marina Bay waterfront loop is a 3.5km walk with constantly changing views. Do it in the early evening — the city lights up beautifully after dark and it's cooler than midday.

Changi Airport — Start Here

Jewel Changi — the newest terminal at Changi Airport — is genuinely worth visiting even if you're not flying. A 40-metre indoor waterfall (the world's tallest), a multi-level indoor forest, dozens of restaurants and shops. Many visitors plan their first hour in Singapore here before heading to the city.

💡 Changi Airport is consistently rated the world's best. Budget an extra 2 hours when departing — explore Jewel, try the hawker food in Terminal 2 or 3, use the free city tours for long layovers (6+ hours).

Cultural Neighbourhoods — The Real Singapore

Chinatown — Singapore's traditional Chinese quarter. Temples, incense, traditional medicine shops, and some of the best hawker food in the city. The Chinatown Complex Food Centre has over 200 stalls. The Buddha Tooth Relic Temple is free and genuinely beautiful inside. Don't miss the streets of Keong Saik Road for the modern café and restaurant scene.

Little India — a kaleidoscope of colour, spice and sound. Sri Veeramakaliamman Temple, flower garland stalls, Tekka Centre market, gold jewellery shops. Serangoon Road has been the heart of Singapore's Indian community for over 150 years. The banana leaf curry restaurants here are some of the best Indian food outside India.

Kampong Glam — the Malay and Muslim quarter, centred on the Sultan Mosque with its golden dome. Arab Street, Haji Lane (Singapore's most Instagrammed alley), and excellent nasi padang restaurants. The Malay Heritage Centre tells the community's story well.

Walk through all three cultural neighbourhoods in a day. This is what makes Singapore different from every other modern city.

Tiong Bahru — Local & Charming

Tiong Bahru is Singapore's most loveable neighbourhood — art deco apartment buildings from the 1930s, independent bookshops, specialty coffee cafés, excellent hawker food at Tiong Bahru Market. Very few tourists, very local feel. A perfect half-day away from the main attractions.

The streets around Kim Cheng and Guan Chuan Streets have Singapore's most charming street-level architecture. The Tiong Bahru Bakery (French-influenced, excellent croissants) is worth a morning visit.

What to Eat in Singapore

Singapore's food scene is one of the world's great ones. Start at the hawker centres and work outward.

🍗 Hainanese Chicken Rice — poached chicken on rice cooked in chicken stock, with ginger sauce and chilli. The national dish. Order it at Tian Tian at Maxwell Food Centre.
🦀 Chilli Crab — Singapore's most iconic dish. Mud crab in a sweet, spicy tomato-based sauce. Expensive but worth it. Order with mantou (fried buns) to soak up the sauce.
🍜 Laksa — spicy coconut curry noodle soup, Peranakan heritage. Rich, complex, addictive. 328 Katong Laksa is the most famous version.
🥩 Satay — grilled skewers of chicken, beef or mutton with peanut sauce. Best at Lau Pa Sat's Satay Street (from 7pm, outdoor tables).
🍳 Char kway teow — stir-fried flat rice noodles with egg, bean sprouts, Chinese sausage and cockles. One of the great hawker dishes.
🥛 Teh tarik — "pulled tea", hot milk tea poured between cups to create froth. The everyday drink of Singapore. Order it everywhere.
💡 Follow the queues at hawker centres — a long queue means a good stall. Some of the best stalls run out by 1pm. Arrive at 11:30am for lunch.

Best Time to Visit Singapore

🌤️ February–April — least rain, lower humidity, most comfortable for walking outdoors
☀️ June–August — hot and dry, good for Sentosa beach days
🌧️ November–January — wetter (northeast monsoon), but still very warm. Indoor attractions shine.
🎆 Chinese New Year (Jan/Feb) — Chinatown transforms with lights, markets and celebrations. One of the best times to visit.

Singapore is equatorial — always hot and humid (28–34°C). Dress light, stay hydrated, use the excellent air-conditioned MRT to avoid walking in midday heat.

Singapore Travel Tips

🚇 MRT + bus — Singapore's public transport is world-class. Get an EZ-Link card at any MRT station. Covers metro, buses and some attractions.
📱 Use Grab (Southeast Asia's Uber) for taxis — reliable, metered, no haggling. Much better than hailing street taxis.
💧 Tap water is safe to drink — Singapore has some of the cleanest tap water in Asia.
🚫 No chewing gum — sale and import is banned. Don't bring it in. Singapore's rules are real and enforced.
💵 Singapore is expensive by Southeast Asian standards but competitive with European cities. Hotels and hawker food are the best value.
🌿 Singapore Botanic Gardens — free UNESCO World Heritage site, excellent for a morning walk away from the city crowds.

⚠️ Only eating at hotel restaurants

Singapore's best food is at hawker centres costing S$4–8 per dish. Eating only at hotel restaurants means missing the entire point of Singapore's food culture.

⚠️ Skipping the cultural neighbourhoods

Many visitors spend all their time in Marina Bay and miss Chinatown, Little India and Kampong Glam entirely. These three areas are what make Singapore genuinely special.

Where to eat
Singapore food picks
Start at the hawker centres. Then go from there. Singapore's food never disappoints.
🍗
Tian Tian Chicken Rice — Maxwell
📍 Maxwell Food Centre, Stall #01-10 · The most famous chicken rice stall in Singapore — poached chicken, fragrant rice, ginger sauce and chilli. Queue at 11:30am before the lunchtime rush. S$5–6 per plate. Michelin Bib Gourmand. This is the national dish done perfectly, at hawker prices. Go once, you'll understand Singapore food completely.
📍 Maps →
🦀
Jumbo Seafood — Chilli Crab
📍 Riverside Point, Clarke Quay · Singapore's most iconic dish — mud crab in a rich, spicy tomato sauce, with fried mantou buns to soak it up. Jumbo is the most reliable version for visitors. Expensive (S$60–100+ for a crab) but worth it once. Book ahead for dinner. The river view terrace is the right table to ask for.
📍 Maps →
🍜
328 Katong Laksa
📍 East Coast Road, Katong · Singapore's most famous laksa — Peranakan-style coconut curry noodle soup, thick and rich, served with cockles. The noodles are cut short so you eat with a spoon only. S$6–8. Always busy. The Katong neighbourhood around it has excellent Peranakan heritage architecture — worth the short trip east.
📍 Maps →
🥩
Lau Pa Sat — Satay Street
📍 Boon Tat Street, CBD · From 7pm nightly, the street beside Lau Pa Sat hawker centre closes to traffic and becomes an outdoor satay street — dozens of vendors, plastic tables and chairs, cold beer, charcoal-grilled chicken and beef skewers with peanut sauce. S$0.80–1 per skewer. One of the most atmospheric evening meals in Singapore.
📍 Maps →
🏆
Singapore's Michelin hawker stalls — world's cheapest Michelin meals Singapore has hawker stalls with Michelin stars. Hawker Chan (soya sauce chicken rice, S$3.80) held a star for years. Hill Street Tai Hwa Pork Noodle still holds one. These aren't restaurants — they're stalls in food centres. Eating Michelin-quality food for under S$10 is a uniquely Singaporean experience and one of the great food moments in Asia.
